## 1.a Non-Invasive and Invasive Methods of Blood Pressure Measurement

### Non-Invasive Method
Measures blood pressure externally without penetrating the body.

#### Auscultatory Method
- Uses an inflatable cuff, a mercury or aneroid manometer, and a stethoscope.
- Based on detection of Korotkoff sounds generated by turbulent blood flow as the cuff deflates.

#### Oscillometric Method
- Commonly used in automatic digital BP monitors.
- Measures tiny oscillations in cuff pressure caused by arterial pressure pulses.

**Advantages**
- Safe
- Painless
- Easy to use
- Zero risk of infection

**Disadvantages**
- Less accurate in critical/shock conditions
- Easily affected by patient movement or ambient noise

> **Highlight:** Non-invasive methods are safe and convenient, but invasive methods are preferred when real-time high accuracy is essential.

### Invasive Method (Direct)
Measures blood pressure by directly entering the artery for highly accurate, continuous monitoring.

**Procedure**
- A fluid-filled catheter is surgically inserted into an artery (like radial or femoral artery).
- The catheter is connected to a strain-gauge pressure transducer and a continuous flushing system (heparinized saline) to prevent clotting.
- The transducer converts mechanical pressure into an electrical waveform.

**Advantages**
- Gold standard for accuracy
- Continuous, beat-to-beat real-time BP measurement
- Essential for ICUs and major surgeries

**Disadvantages**
- Painful
- Complex setup
- Requires highly skilled personnel
- Risk of infection, bleeding, or thrombosis

## 1.b Blood Flow Measurement Using Sine Wave Flow Meter and Doppler Flow Meter

### Sine Wave Electromagnetic Flow Meter

**Principle**
Based on Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction:

E = B * l * v

When conductive blood flows with velocity v through a magnetic field B, a voltage E is induced across vessel diameter l, proportional to flow velocity.

**Working**
- An electromagnet applies a sinusoidal (AC) magnetic field.
- AC is used instead of DC to prevent electrode polarization and eliminate DC drift potentials.
- Electrodes in contact with the vessel wall detect the induced alternating voltage.

**Advantages**
- High accuracy
- Linear output
- Independent of blood viscosity or flow profile

**Disadvantages**
- Highly invasive (surgical exposure required)
- Expensive
- Susceptible to transformer noise (quadrature voltage)

### Ultrasonic Doppler Flow Meter

**Principle**
Based on the Doppler effect. Frequency shift (delta f) of reflected ultrasound from moving RBCs is proportional to blood velocity.

Doppler equation:

delta f = (2 * f_t * v * cos(theta)) / c

**Working**
- Piezoelectric probe transmits high-frequency ultrasound (2-10 MHz) through skin into vessel.
- Reflected waves from moving RBCs are received.
- Frequency shift is used to calculate velocity.

**Advantages**
- Completely non-invasive
- Safe
- Widely used clinically
- Suitable for continuous transcutaneous monitoring

**Disadvantages**
- Accuracy strongly depends on probe angle (theta)
- If theta = 90 degrees, cos(90) = 0, so no flow is detected

> **Highlight:** Doppler flow meter is the most common non-invasive blood-flow measurement method.

## 1.c 12-Lead ECG System and Brain Waves with Frequency

### 12-Lead ECG System
A standard 12-lead ECG uses 10 physical electrodes to view heart electrical activity from 12 vector perspectives.

- **3 Bipolar Limb Leads:** Lead I, Lead II, Lead III (Einthoven's Triangle)
- **3 Unipolar Augmented Limb Leads:** aVR, aVL, aVF
- **6 Unipolar Chest (Precordial) Leads:** V1, V2, V3, V4, V5, V6

### Brain Waves and Frequency (EEG)

| Brain Wave | Frequency | Typical Condition |
|---|---|---|
| Delta | 0.5-4 Hz | Deep, dreamless sleep or severe brain disorders |
| Theta | 4-8 Hz | Drowsiness, light sleep, emotional stress |
| Alpha | 8-13 Hz | Relaxed awake state (eyes closed) |
| Beta | 13-30 Hz | Active thinking, concentration, alertness |

> **Highlight:** Alpha waves indicate relaxed wakefulness, while beta waves indicate active mental activity.

## 1.d Principle of X-Ray Generation, Soft vs Hard X-Rays, and Imaging Differences

### Principle of X-Ray Generation
X-rays are produced when high-speed electrons collide with a dense metal target.

- In an X-ray tube, a cathode filament emits electrons (thermionic emission).
- High accelerating voltage (kV) drives electrons toward a high atomic number anode target (usually tungsten).
- Sudden deceleration at target converts kinetic energy into:
  - X-ray photons (Bremsstrahlung)
  - Heat (more than 99%)

### Soft vs Hard X-Rays

- **Soft X-rays:** Lower energy, longer wavelength, low penetration, easily absorbed by soft tissue (example: mammography)
- **Hard X-rays:** Higher energy, shorter wavelength, high penetration, useful for chest and bone imaging

### Difference: X-Ray vs CAT (CT)

| Feature | X-Ray Imaging | CAT (CT) Imaging |
|---|---|---|
| Image type | 2D flat projection | 3D cross-sectional slices |
| Detail | Less detail (overlapping structures) | Highly detailed |
| Principle | Single stationary X-ray burst | Rotating X-ray tube, multiple projections |
| Time | Very fast | Slower than standard X-ray |
| Cost | Low | High |

### Difference: X-Ray Imaging vs Nuclear Imaging

| Feature | X-Ray Imaging | Nuclear Imaging |
|---|---|---|
| Radiation source | External machine (X-ray tube) | Radioisotopes inside body |
| Direction | Outside to inside | Inside to outside |
| Clinical focus | Anatomy (structure) | Function (metabolism, blood flow, active tumors) |
| Examples | Chest X-ray, Dental X-ray | PET, SPECT |

## 1.e MRI and USG System Differences with Working Principle

### MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)

**Principle**
MRI works on Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR).

- Hydrogen protons align in a strong magnetic field.
- RF pulse disturbs alignment.
- When RF is removed, protons realign and emit RF signals.
- Receiver coils detect these signals to form detailed cross-sectional images.

### USG (Ultrasonography)

**Principle**
USG works on the piezoelectric effect.

- Probe has piezoelectric crystals.
- Electrical pulse makes crystals vibrate and emit high-frequency ultrasound.
- Echoes return from tissue boundaries.
- Returning echoes generate electrical signals to form real-time images.

### MRI vs USG Comparison

| Feature | MRI System | USG System |
|---|---|---|
| Principle | Nuclear Magnetic Resonance | Piezoelectric effect |
| Radiation | Magnetic field + RF waves (no ionizing radiation) | Sound waves (no ionizing radiation) |
| Image output | Detailed static/cross-sectional soft tissue images | Real-time dynamic imaging |
| Cost and size | Very expensive, large, non-portable | Lower cost, portable |
| Applications | Brain, spinal cord, tumors, joints | Pregnancy, echocardiography, blood flow |

## 1.f Heart Action Potential, ECG Rhythm Characteristics, Bradycardia and Tachycardia

### Origin of Cardiac Action Potential
The action potential originates from the **Sinoatrial (SA) node**, the natural pacemaker in the right atrium.

### ECG Rhythm Components

- **P wave:** Atrial depolarization
- **QRS complex:** Ventricular depolarization (atrial repolarization hidden)
- **T wave:** Ventricular repolarization
- **PR interval:** Delay between atrial and ventricular activation (normal 0.12-0.20 s)
- **ST segment:** Early ventricular repolarization; normally isoelectric

### Bradycardia vs Tachycardia

- **Bradycardia:** Resting heart rate less than 60 bpm
- **Tachycardia:** Resting heart rate greater than 100 bpm

> **Highlight:** SA node sets the heart rhythm; changes in rate define bradycardia or tachycardia.

## 1.g Defibrillator vs Pacemaker and Synchronous vs Asynchronous Pacemaker

### Defibrillator vs Pacemaker

| Feature | Defibrillator | Pacemaker |
|---|---|---|
| Primary purpose | Restores rhythm during fatal arrest (example: VF) | Maintains adequate regular rate in slow rhythm patients |
| Energy delivery | High-energy shock | Low-energy pacing pulses |
| Usage | Emergency, acute life-saving | Long-term rhythm management |
| Operation mode | Triggered manually/automatically | Continuous over years |
| Placement | Mostly external (ICD also exists) | Usually implantable |

### Synchronous vs Asynchronous Pacemaker

#### Synchronous Pacemaker (Demand Pacemaker)
- Senses intrinsic activity (R-wave)
- Delivers pulse only when heart fails to beat in set time
- More efficient, conserves battery, avoids dangerous R-on-T pacing

#### Asynchronous Pacemaker (Fixed-Rate Pacemaker)
- Delivers pulses at fixed rate regardless of intrinsic heart rhythm
- No sensing circuitry
- May compete with natural rhythm and can trigger dangerous arrhythmias

> **Highlight:** Demand (synchronous) pacemakers are safer for most clinical use than fixed-rate asynchronous pacing.
